Two expressions can look different while behaving identically in every semantic case.

That is the idea behind logical equivalence.

## Equivalence

Two sentences are logically equivalent when they receive the same truth value under every possible assignment of truth values to their component sentences.

The usual notation is:

$$
p \equiv q
$$

This is stronger than saying that two sentences happen to agree in one example. It says their behavior matches in every case.

## Truth Tables and Equivalence

Truth tables give an early method for checking equivalence.

If the final columns of two expressions match row by row, then the expressions are logically equivalent.

This is one reason the course introduces tables before the first formal rewrite proofs. The tables reveal the semantic target.

## Tautology and Contradiction

An expression is a tautology when it is always true.

An expression is a contradiction when it is always false.

In the notation of this unit:

$$
p \text{ is a tautology } \iff p \equiv \top
$$

$$
p \text{ is a contradiction } \iff p \equiv \bot
$$

These classifications will show up repeatedly in early logic problems.

## Why Equivalence Matters

Logical equivalence is not only a semantic classification. It is also the basis of a proof method.

Once the course starts proving that one expression can be rewritten into another by named laws, the hidden target is often equivalence.

That is why this note sits between truth tables and proof-writing.
