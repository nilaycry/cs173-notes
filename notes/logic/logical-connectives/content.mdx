Logical connectives are the operations that build compound sentences from simpler ones.

Once sentences carry truth values, connectives tell us how to produce new truth-valued sentences from old ones.

## Negation

Negation reverses the truth value of a sentence.

If $p$ is true, then $\neg p$ is false.

If $p$ is false, then $\neg p$ is true.

So negation does not create a new topic out of nowhere. It acts on a sentence that already has a truth value and produces its opposite.

## Conjunction and Disjunction

The conjunction $p \land q$ asserts that both component sentences hold.

The disjunction $p \lor q$ asserts that at least one of them holds.

These are the first examples where the course starts building larger logical objects out of smaller ones.

## Implication

Implication is usually the first connective that feels less natural in symbolic form than it does in ordinary English.

The expression $p \to q$ should be read as a logical condition, not as a claim about causation or chronology.

That distinction matters because later proof methods, especially direct proof and contrapositive reasoning, depend on reading implication correctly.

## Biconditional

The biconditional $p \leftrightarrow q$ records that the two sentences have matching truth behavior in both directions.

It is stronger than implication. It says each sentence holds exactly when the other does.
