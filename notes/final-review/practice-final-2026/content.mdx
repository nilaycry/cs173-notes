This practice final is not asking for one new trick. It is asking whether the course's main habits have stuck: parse the statement, name the objects, choose the right definition, and then write the proof in the style that topic expects.

The exam is scary mostly because it mixes units. That is normal for CS 173. A graph problem may use induction. A countability problem may be about strings. A true/false question may be testing whether a theorem says exactly what you think it says.

## The Shape of the Final

The practice final has six parts.

1. True/false concept checks.
2. Short-answer constructions.
3. A formal zeroth-order logic proof.
4. An induction proof about integer linear combinations.
5. A proof that there are infinitely many finite binary strings.
6. An induction proof that every connected graph contains a spanning tree.

The big idea is that each part tests a different kind of mathematical reflex. Do not use the same writing style everywhere.

## Part 1: True/False Checks

True/false questions are precision traps. Do not answer from vibe. Translate the statement into the relevant theorem, then look for a counterexample if anything feels too strong.

The main facts being tested are:

- odd squares are congruent to $1$ modulo $4$;
- countable does not mean finite;
- the handshaking theorem controls odd-degree vertices, not even-degree vertices;
- if $x=5a$ and $y=5b$ for nonzero integers $a,b$, then $\gcd(x,y)=5\gcd(a,b)$, so the gcd is at least $5$;
- power-set inclusion tracks subset inclusion;
- taking $B=\varnothing$ is allowed when a problem only says $B\subseteq A$;
- propositions are finite strings over a finite logical alphabet, so there are countably many of them;
- $n$ vertices and $n-1$ edges do not guarantee connectedness;
- modular equations can be simplified by reducing coefficients;
- a finite tree satisfies $|V(T)|=|E(T)|+1$.

## Part 2: Short-Answer Constructions

Short-answer construction problems reward explicit examples. You do not need a paragraph when a clean object and one line of justification will do.

For finite strings over the alphabet $\{0,1,2\}$, group strings by length. If

$$
T\coloneqq \{\,t\mid (\exists n\in\mathbb{N})(t:n\to\{0,1,2\})\,\},
$$

then one useful bijection $\varphi:T\to\mathbb{N}$ is

$$
\varphi(t)
\coloneqq
\frac{3^n-1}{2}+\sum_{i<n}t(i)3^i,
$$

where $n$ is the length of $t$. The offset

$$
\frac{3^n-1}{2}=\sum_{j<n}3^j
$$

puts all strings of length $n$ after all shorter strings, and the base-$3$ sum orders the strings of that fixed length.

For the other short-answer prompts, keep these examples in mind:

- an $\in$-transitive set that is not a natural number: $\mathbb{N}$;
- an uncountable set that is not equal to any power set: $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})\setminus\{\varnothing\}$;
- the fewest edges in a connected graph on $n\in\mathbb{N}^+$ vertices: $n-1$;
- a connected graph where at least three edges must be removed to disconnect it: $K_4$.

## Part 3: Formal Logic Proof

The statement is

$$
\vdash (\neg q\wedge (p\to q))\to \neg p.
$$

Read it as a formal version of modus tollens. The proof outline is:

1. Assume $\neg q\wedge(p\to q)$.
2. Use conjunction elimination to get $\neg q$ and $p\to q$.
3. Assume $p$.
4. Use modus ponens on $p$ and $p\to q$ to get $q$.
5. Combine $q$ with $\neg q$ to get a contradiction.
6. Discharge the assumption $p$ to prove $\neg p$.
7. Discharge the first assumption to prove the implication.

The page is not asking for an informal English proof. It explicitly asks you to cite the logic axioms, rules of inference, and theorems. The answer should look like a derivation, not a paragraph.

7. $\neg p$, from 4 through 6 by negation introduction.

## Part 4: Induction With Integer Combinations

The statement is

$$
(\forall n\in\mathbb{N})(n\geq 4\Rightarrow(\exists x,y\in\mathbb{Z})(2x+5y=n)).
$$

Because $x$ and $y$ are allowed to be integers, not natural numbers, the induction step can add $1$ by using

$$
1=5-2\cdot 2.
$$

A clean proof uses base case $n=4$:

$$
4=2\cdot 2+5\cdot 0.
$$

For the step, assume

$$
n=2x+5y.
$$

Then

$$
n+1
=2x+5y+1
=2(x-2)+5(y+1).
$$

Since $x-2$ and $y+1$ are integers, the induction step preserves the required form.

## Part 5: Infinitely Many Finite Binary Strings

The set of finite binary strings is

$$
\{\,s\mid(\exists n\in\mathbb{N})(s:n\to\{0,1\})\,\}.
$$

To prove this set is infinite, it is enough to inject $\mathbb{N}$ into it. Send each $n$ to the all-zero string of length $n$:

$$
z_n:n\to\{0,1\},
\qquad
z_n(i)\coloneqq 0.
$$

Different lengths give different functions, since the domains are different. So the map $n\mapsto z_n$ is injective, and therefore there are infinitely many finite binary strings.

This is a good example of the course's cardinality habit: do not try to "count forever" by arithmetic. Build a function.

## Part 6: Connected Graphs Contain Spanning Trees

The statement is a graph-induction theorem:

> **Theorem.** Every finite connected graph contains a spanning tree.

The proof should use the cycle-removal idea.

If the connected graph $G$ has no cycle, then $G$ is already a tree, so it is its own spanning tree.

If $G$ has a cycle, choose an edge $e$ on that cycle. Removing $e$ does not disconnect the graph, because the rest of the cycle still gives a route between the endpoints of $e$. Thus $G-e$ is connected, has the same vertex set, and has fewer edges. By induction, $G-e$ contains a spanning tree. Since $G-e$ is a subgraph of $G$, that same spanning tree is also a spanning tree of $G$.

The important sentence is not "remove a cycle edge." The important sentence is "removing an edge from a cycle preserves connectedness." That is the theorem doing the work.

## What To Review Before Trying It Cold

Review these pages in this order:

1. Logic proof rules and proof-writing expectations.
2. Cardinality through functions.
3. Countable sets and strings.
4. Pigeonhole and finite counting algebra.
5. Graph degrees and the degree-sum theorem.
6. Connected graphs and trees.
7. Spanning trees.

When you retake the practice final, use this timing:

- Part 1: 15 minutes.
- Part 2: 20 minutes.
- Part 3: 15 minutes.
- Part 4: 15 minutes.
- Part 5: 10 minutes.
- Part 6: 20 minutes.
- Review pass: 15 minutes.

The review pass matters. Most lost points on this kind of exam come from a missing quantifier, a function that was described but not defined, or an induction step that changes the object without proving the hypothesis still applies.
