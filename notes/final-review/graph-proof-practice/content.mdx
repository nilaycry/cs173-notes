These problems are meant for the final-review stage. You may assume the graph theorems already proved in notes, lectures, and problem sets, including the degree-sum theorem, parity of odd-degree vertices, unique paths in trees, tree edge count, leaves in trees, cut-edge facts, and the spanning-tree theorem.

That does not mean every proof should be one sentence. The work is choosing the right theorem, checking its hypotheses, and translating it back to the graph in front of you.

## How To Use These

Try each proof before opening the hint. If you get stuck, ask which earlier theorem sounds closest to the conclusion:

- degree statement: use degree-sum, parity, or pigeonhole;
- tree statement: use connected plus acyclic, unique paths, leaves, or $|V(T)|=|E(T)|+1$;
- edge-removal statement: ask whether the edge lies on a cycle;
- connectedness statement: try components, spanning trees, or contrapositive.

## Problem 1: Same Degree Vertices

Prove that every graph with at least two vertices contains two distinct vertices with the same degree.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

Let $G$ be a graph with at least two vertices. Write $|V(G)|=n$, so $n\geq 2$.

For each $v\in V(G)$, the degree of $v$ is at most $n-1$, since $v$ can be adjacent only to vertices in $V(G)\setminus\{v\}$. Therefore every degree lies in

$$
\{\,d\in\mathbb{N}\mid d\leq n-1\,\}.
$$

The two extreme values $0$ and $n-1$ cannot both occur. Indeed, if some vertex has degree $n-1$, then it is adjacent to every other vertex, so no vertex has degree $0$.

Let

$$
B\coloneqq \{\,\deg_G(v)\mid v\in V(G)\,\}
$$

be the set of actual degree values. Since every degree is a natural number at most $n-1$, and the two extreme values $0$ and $n-1$ cannot both occur, we have $|B|\leq n-1$.

Now regard the degree function as a function

$$
\deg_G:V(G)\to B.
$$

The domain has $n$ elements, while the codomain has at most $n-1$ elements. By the pigeonhole principle, this function is not injective. Hence there exist distinct vertices $u,v\in V(G)$ such that

$$
\deg_G(u)=\deg_G(v).
$$

Therefore every graph with at least two vertices contains two distinct vertices with the same degree.
</details>

## Problem 2: Large Minimum Degree Forces Connectedness

Let $G$ be a graph with $|V(G)|=n$. Prove that if

$$
(\forall v\in V(G))\left(\deg_G(v)\geq \frac{n-1}{2}\right),
$$

then $G$ is connected.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

We prove the contrapositive. Suppose $G$ is disconnected. Then $G$ has at least two connected components.

Choose a connected component $C$ of smallest order, and let

$$
m\coloneqq |V(C)|.
$$

Since there is at least one other component and $C$ has smallest order,

$$
m\leq \frac n2.
$$

Choose $v\in V(C)$. No edge can join $v$ to a vertex outside $C$, because then that outside vertex would belong to the same connected component as $v$. Therefore every neighbor of $v$ lies in $V(C)\setminus\{v\}$, so

$$
\deg_G(v)\leq m-1.
$$

Using $m\leq n/2$, we get

$$
\deg_G(v)\leq m-1\leq \frac n2-1=\frac{n-2}{2}<\frac{n-1}{2}.
$$

Thus, if $G$ is disconnected, then there exists $v\in V(G)$ such that

$$
\deg_G(v)<\frac{n-1}{2}.
$$

This is the contrapositive of the desired statement. Hence if every vertex has degree at least $(n-1)/2$, then $G$ is connected.
</details>

## Problem 3: Cut Edges Are Not on Cycles

Let $G$ be connected and let $e\in E(G)$. Prove that if $e$ is a cut edge, then $e$ lies on no cycle in $G$.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

We prove the contrapositive. Suppose $e$ lies on a cycle in $G$.

Since $G$ is connected and $e$ lies on a cycle, the cycle-edge deletion theorem says that $G-e$ is connected. Therefore $e$ is not a cut edge.

So, if $e$ is a cut edge, then $e$ cannot lie on any cycle in $G$.
</details>

## Problem 4: Non-Cut Edges Lie on Cycles

Let $G$ be connected and let $e\in E(G)$. Prove that if $G-e$ is connected, then $e$ lies on a cycle in $G$.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

Let $e=\{u,v\}$. Since $G-e$ is connected, there is a path in $G-e$ from $u$ to $v$.

Let that path be

$$
p:u\leadsto v.
$$

Because $p$ is a path in $G-e$, it does not use the edge $e$. In the original graph $G$, the edge $e=\{u,v\}$ is present. Thus the path $p$ together with the edge $e$ forms a cycle in $G$.

Therefore $e$ lies on a cycle in $G$.
</details>

## Problem 5: Connected Graph With $n-1$ Edges

Let $G$ be a connected graph with $|V(G)|=n$ and $|E(G)|=n-1$. Prove that $G$ is a tree.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

Since $G$ is connected, the spanning-tree theorem gives a spanning tree $T$ of $G$. Since $T$ is spanning,

$$
V(T)=V(G),
$$

so $|V(T)|=n$. Since $T$ is a tree,

$$
|E(T)|=|V(T)|-1=n-1.
$$

Since $T$ is a subgraph of $G$, we have $E(T)\subseteq E(G)$. But

$$
|E(T)|=n-1=|E(G)|.
$$

So $E(T)=E(G)$. We also have $V(T)=V(G)$, so $T=G$.

Since $T$ is a tree and $T=G$, the graph $G$ is a tree.
</details>

## Problem 6: Exactly One Cycle

Suppose $G$ is connected and contains exactly one cycle. Prove that

$$
|E(G)|=|V(G)|.
$$

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

Let $C$ be the unique cycle in $G$, and choose an edge $e\in E(C)$.

Since $e$ lies on a cycle and $G$ is connected, the graph $G-e$ is connected.

We claim that $G-e$ is acyclic. If $G-e$ had a cycle $C'$, then $C'$ would also be a cycle in $G$. Since $e$ is not an edge of $G-e$, the cycle $C'$ does not use $e$. But the original cycle $C$ does use $e$, so $C'\neq C$. This would give two distinct cycles in $G$, contradicting the assumption that $G$ contains exactly one cycle.

Thus $G-e$ is a tree. Therefore

$$
|E(G-e)|=|V(G-e)|-1.
$$

Since deleting $e$ does not change the vertex set,

$$
|E(G)|-1=|V(G)|-1.
$$

Hence $|E(G)|=|V(G)|$.
</details>

## Problem 7: Adding One Edge to a Tree

Let $T$ be a tree, and let $u,v\in V(T)$ be distinct non-adjacent vertices. Let $G$ be the graph obtained from $T$ by adding the edge $\{u,v\}$. Prove that $G$ contains exactly one cycle.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

Since $T$ is a tree, there is a unique path in $T$ from $u$ to $v$. Call this path $p$.

The graph $G$ is obtained by adding the edge $\{u,v\}$ to $T$. The path $p$ together with the new edge $\{u,v\}$ forms a cycle in $G$. Hence $G$ contains at least one cycle.

Now let $C$ be any cycle in $G$. Since $T$ is acyclic, $C$ cannot use only edges from $T$. Therefore $C$ must use the new edge $\{u,v\}$.

If we remove the edge $\{u,v\}$ from $C$, what remains is a path in $T$ from $u$ to $v$. But $T$ has a unique path from $u$ to $v$, namely $p$. Therefore every cycle in $G$ consists of $p$ together with the edge $\{u,v\}$.

Thus $G$ contains exactly one cycle.
</details>

## Problem 8: Minimum Degree At Least Two

Prove that every nonempty finite graph in which every vertex has degree at least $2$ contains a cycle.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

Assume for contradiction that $G$ contains no cycle. Then $G$ is acyclic, so each connected component of $G$ is a tree.

Since $G$ is nonempty, it has at least one connected component. Choose a component $C$.

The component $C$ cannot consist of a single isolated vertex, because every vertex of $G$ has degree at least $2$. Hence $C$ is a tree with at least two vertices.

Every tree with at least two vertices has a leaf, so there exists $v\in V(C)$ such that

$$
\deg_C(v)=1.
$$

No edge leaves a connected component, so the degree of $v$ in $G$ is the same as its degree in $C$. Thus

$$
\deg_G(v)=1,
$$

contradicting the assumption that every vertex of $G$ has degree at least $2$.

Thus $G$ must contain a cycle.
</details>

## Problem 9: Edges in a Tree Are Cut Edges

Let $T$ be a tree. Prove that every edge of $T$ is a cut edge.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

Let $e=\{u,v\}\in E(T)$. Since $T$ is a tree, there is a unique path from $u$ to $v$. That path must be the edge $e$ itself.

Suppose, for contradiction, that $T-e$ is connected. Then there is a path from $u$ to $v$ in $T-e$.

This path does not use the edge $e$. But in $T$, the edge $e$ itself is also a path from $u$ to $v$. Therefore $T$ would contain two distinct paths from $u$ to $v$, contradicting the unique-path theorem for trees.

Therefore $T-e$ is disconnected, so $e$ is a cut edge.
</details>

## Problem 10: Complete Graph Edge Count

Let $K_n$ be the complete graph on $n$ vertices. Prove that

$$
|E(K_n)|=\frac{n(n-1)}{2}.
$$

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

In $K_n$, every vertex is adjacent to the other $n-1$ vertices, so every vertex has degree $n-1$.

By the degree-sum theorem,

$$
\sum_{v\in V(K_n)}\deg_{K_n}(v)=2|E(K_n)|.
$$

The left side is

$$
n(n-1).
$$

Therefore

$$
2|E(K_n)|=n(n-1),
$$

so

$$
|E(K_n)|=\frac{n(n-1)}{2}.
$$
</details>

## Problem 11: A Connected Bipartition Edge Bound

Let $G$ be a connected graph whose vertex set is partitioned into two nonempty sets $A$ and $B$, and suppose every edge has one endpoint in $A$ and one endpoint in $B$. Prove that if $|A|=1$, then $G$ has a vertex adjacent to every vertex in $B$.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

Let $A=\{a\}$. Since $G$ is connected, no vertex in $B$ is isolated.

Fix $b\in B$. Since $b$ has some neighbor and every edge has one endpoint in $A$ and one endpoint in $B$, every neighbor of $b$ must lie in $A$. But $A$ has only one element, namely $a$.

Thus $b$ is adjacent to $a$. Since $b\in B$ was arbitrary, $a$ is adjacent to every vertex in $B$.
</details>

## Problem 12: Trees Have a Low-Degree Vertex

Prove that every finite tree has a vertex of degree at most $1$.

<details>
<summary>Full proof</summary>

Let $T$ be a finite tree.

If $T$ has one vertex, then that vertex has degree $0$, so $T$ has a vertex of degree at most $1$.

If $T$ has at least two vertices, then the leaves theorem says that $T$ has a leaf. By definition, a leaf has degree $1$. Therefore $T$ again has a vertex of degree at most $1$.

In either case, the tree has a vertex of degree at most $1$.
</details>

## What These Are Training

These are the proof moves that keep repeating:

- use pigeonhole on the degree function;
- prove connectedness by contradiction or contrapositive;
- delete an edge only after checking whether it lies on a cycle;
- use spanning trees to convert connected graph statements into tree statements;
- use unique paths when adding or removing edges in trees;
- use degree-sum when a question asks for a count of edges or parity.

For a timed final, the point is not to invent graph theory from scratch. The point is to recognize which proved theorem is supposed to unlock the problem, then write the two or three sentences that make the theorem apply.
