This practice final is modeled on the local practice midterms and the practice final worksheet. The questions are new, but the habits are the same: parse quantifiers carefully, expand definitions, build functions for cardinality claims, and use the correct proof mode for the topic.

Use it in two passes. First, try the questions cold. Then open the self-checks and compare proof structure, not just final answers.

## Part 1: True or False

Mark each statement true or false. Do not prove your answers on a timed attempt, but make sure you can explain the reason afterward.

1. Every sentence with a truth value is a proposition.
2. The sentence "This sentence is false" has a truth value.
3. For every set $X$, $\varnothing\subseteq X$.
4. For all sets $A$ and $B$, if $\mathcal{P}(A)\subseteq\mathcal{P}(B)$, then $A\subseteq B$.
5. For all sets $A$ and $B$, if $A\subseteq B$, then $|A|\leq |B|$.
6. If $X$ and $Y$ are finite, $|X|=|Y|$, and $f:X\to Y$ is injective, then $f$ is surjective.
7. The set of all finite binary strings is uncountable.
8. The set of all infinite binary strings is countable.
9. If $X$ is finite and $|X|=n$, then $|\mathcal{P}(X)|=2^n$.
10. If a graph has $n$ vertices and $n-1$ edges, then it is connected.
11. Every finite graph has an even number of odd-degree vertices.
12. Every finite tree with at least two vertices has at least two leaves.
13. If $e$ is an edge on a cycle in a connected graph $G$, then $G-e$ is connected.
14. Every finite connected graph contains a spanning tree.
15. If $p$ is prime and $p\mid ab$, then $p\mid a$ and $p\mid b$.
16. For every $n\in\mathbb{N}$, $5\mid 9^n-4^n$.
17. For every $n\in\mathbb{N}$, $n$ and $n+1$ are coprime.
18. If $A$ is countably infinite, then $\mathcal{P}(A)$ is countable.

<details>
<summary>Answer key for Part 1</summary>

1. True. A proposition is a sentence with a truth value.
2. False. This is the standard self-reference trap.
3. True. The empty set has no element that could violate subset inclusion.
4. True. Since $A\in\mathcal{P}(A)$, the assumption gives $A\in\mathcal{P}(B)$, so $A\subseteq B$.
5. True. The inclusion map $A\to B$ is injective.
6. True. For finite sets of the same size, an injection cannot miss an output.
7. False. Finite binary strings can be listed by length.
8. False. Infinite binary strings are uncountable by diagonalization.
9. True. Each element is either included or not included.
10. False. A disconnected graph can have $n-1$ edges.
11. True. The degree sum is even, so the number of odd summands is even.
12. True, with the hypothesis "at least two vertices."
13. True. The rest of the cycle still connects the endpoints of $e$.
14. True. Repeatedly delete cycle edges, or prove by induction on edges.
15. False. Euclid's lemma says $p\mid a$ or $p\mid b$.
16. True. Since $9\equiv 4\pmod 5$, their $n$th powers are congruent modulo $5$.
17. True. Any common divisor of consecutive integers divides their difference, which is $1$.
18. False. Cantor's theorem gives $|A|<|\mathcal{P}(A)|$.
</details>

## Part 2: Proof Questions

Write complete proofs. These are not meant to be solved by quoting a sentence from memory. Each one is testing whether you can put the right definitions on the page.

### Problem 1: Formal Logic

For propositions $p,q,r$, prove

$$
p\vee q,\quad p\to r,\quad q\to r\vdash r.
$$

Your proof should explicitly cite the zeroth-order logic rules or theorems used.

<details>
<summary>Proof skeleton</summary>

Use disjunction elimination.

1. Assume $p\vee q$.
2. Assume $p\to r$.
3. Assume $q\to r$.
4. From $p$ and $p\to r$, derive $r$ by modus ponens.
5. From $q$ and $q\to r$, derive $r$ by modus ponens.
6. Since $p\vee q$, and both cases imply $r$, conclude $r$ by disjunction elimination.

The important citation is the disjunction-elimination rule:

$$
p\vee q,\quad p\to r,\quad q\to r\vdash r.
$$
</details>

### Problem 2: Power Sets and Subsets

Prove that for all sets $A$ and $B$,

$$
A\subseteq B\Longleftrightarrow \mathcal{P}(A)\subseteq\mathcal{P}(B).
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof skeleton</summary>

Prove both directions.

Forward direction: assume $A\subseteq B$. Let $S\in\mathcal{P}(A)$. Then $S\subseteq A$. Since $A\subseteq B$, transitivity of $\subseteq$ gives $S\subseteq B$. Hence $S\in\mathcal{P}(B)$.

Backward direction: assume $\mathcal{P}(A)\subseteq\mathcal{P}(B)$. Since $A\subseteq A$, we have $A\in\mathcal{P}(A)$. Therefore $A\in\mathcal{P}(B)$. By definition of power set, $A\subseteq B$.
</details>

### Problem 3: Induction and Divisibility

Use induction to prove

$$
(\forall n\in\mathbb{N})(5\mid 9^n-4^n).
$$

<details>
<summary>Proof skeleton</summary>

Base case: for $n=0$,

$$
9^0-4^0=1-1=0,
$$

so $5\mid 0$.

Inductive step: assume $5\mid 9^k-4^k$. Then there exists $a\in\mathbb{Z}$ such that

$$
9^k-4^k=5a.
$$

Now compute

$$
9^{k+1}-4^{k+1}
=9\cdot 9^k-4\cdot 4^k
=9(9^k-4^k)+5\cdot 4^k.
$$

By the inductive hypothesis, $9(9^k-4^k)=45a$, and $45a+5\cdot 4^k$ is divisible by $5$.
</details>

### Problem 4: Infinitely Many Finite Binary Strings

Let

$$
B\coloneqq \{\,s\mid(\exists n\in\mathbb{N})(s:n\to\{0,1\})\,\}.
$$

Prove that $B$ is infinite.

<details>
<summary>Proof skeleton</summary>

Construct an injection $\mathbb{N}\to B$.

For each $n\in\mathbb{N}$, define

$$
z_n:n\to\{0,1\}
$$

by

$$
z_n(i)\coloneqq 0.
$$

Define $F:\mathbb{N}\to B$ by $F(n)\coloneqq z_n$.

If $F(m)=F(n)$, then $z_m=z_n$. Equal functions have equal domains, so $m=n$. Therefore $F$ is injective. Since $\mathbb{N}$ injects into $B$, the set $B$ is infinite.
</details>

### Problem 5: Finite Trinary Strings Are Countable

Let

$$
T\coloneqq \{\,t\mid(\exists n\in\mathbb{N})(t:n\to\{0,1,2\})\,\}.
$$

Prove that $T$ is countable.

<details>
<summary>Proof skeleton</summary>

For each $n\in\mathbb{N}$, let

$$
T_n\coloneqq \{\,t:n\to\{0,1,2\}\,\}.
$$

Then $T_n$ is finite and $|T_n|=3^n$. Also,

$$
T=\bigcup_{n\in\mathbb{N}}T_n.
$$

So $T$ is a countable union of finite sets. Equivalently, list all strings of length $0$, then all strings of length $1$, then length $2$, and continue. This gives an enumeration of $T$, so $T$ is countable.

If an explicit injection is desired, send a string $t:n\to\{0,1,2\}$ to

$$
\frac{3^n-1}{2}+\sum_{i<n}t(i)3^i.
$$

This records the length block and the base-$3$ value within that block.
</details>

### Problem 6: Odd-Degree Vertices

Prove that every finite graph has an even number of odd-degree vertices.

<details>
<summary>Proof skeleton</summary>

Use the degree-sum theorem:

$$
\sum_{v\in V(G)}\deg_G(v)=2|E(G)|.
$$

The right side is even. Split the sum into degrees of even-degree vertices and degrees of odd-degree vertices. The sum of the even degrees is even. Therefore the sum of the odd degrees is also even.

A sum of odd integers is even exactly when there are an even number of odd summands. Hence the number of odd-degree vertices is even.
</details>

### Problem 7: Spanning Trees

Use induction on the number of edges to prove that every finite connected graph contains a spanning tree.

<details>
<summary>Proof skeleton</summary>

Induct on $m=|E(G)|$.

Base case: if $G$ is connected with $0$ edges, then $G$ has one vertex and is already a tree.

Inductive step: let $G$ be connected with $m+1$ edges.

If $G$ has no cycle, then $G$ is connected and acyclic, so it is a tree and is its own spanning tree.

If $G$ has a cycle, choose an edge $e$ on that cycle. Let $H=G-e$. Removing an edge on a cycle preserves connectedness, so $H$ is connected. Also $H$ has the same vertex set as $G$ and has $m$ edges. By the inductive hypothesis, $H$ contains a spanning tree $T$. Since $H$ is a subgraph of $G$ and $V(H)=V(G)$, the same $T$ is a spanning tree of $G$.
</details>

## How To Grade Yourself

A proof earns full credit only if it names the object doing the work.

- Logic proof: cite the inference rule.
- Set proof: expand membership in $\mathcal{P}(A)$.
- Divisibility proof: show the expression is $5$ times an integer.
- String proof: define the function or enumeration.
- Graph proof: explain why the smaller graph is still connected.

If you can do Problems 4 and 5 without looking, your string/countability weakness is turning into a strength. Those are the exact moves this final likes to test.
