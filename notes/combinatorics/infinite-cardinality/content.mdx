Finite cardinality behaves the way ordinary counting trains us to expect. Infinite cardinality asks us to stop trusting that instinct and return to functions. Infinite sets can have the same cardinality as proper subsets, and that is not a paradox in this system. It is the system doing its job.

## Finite and Infinite Sets

A set $F$ is **finite** if there exists $n\in\mathbb{N}$ such that

$$
|F|=|n|.
$$

A set $X$ is **infinite** if it is not finite.

Equivalently, $X$ is infinite if

$$
(\forall n\in\mathbb{N})(|X|\neq |n|).
$$

## Natural Numbers Are Infinite

> **Theorem.** The set $\mathbb{N}$ is infinite.

One proof is by contradiction. If $\mathbb{N}$ were finite, there would be a bijection

$$
f:n\to\mathbb{N}
$$

for some $n\in\mathbb{N}$. Since $f$ lists only finitely many natural numbers, one can form a natural number larger than every value in the list. That number would not be hit by $f$, contradicting surjectivity.

## Hilbert-Hotel Behavior

The set $\mathbb{N}$ has the same cardinality as the positive natural numbers. In finite counting, removing an element makes a set smaller. Here the shift map absorbs that loss:

> **Theorem.**
>
> $$
> |\mathbb{N}|=|\mathbb{N}^+|.
> $$

**Proof.** Define

$$
s:\mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{N}^+
$$

by

$$
s(n)\coloneqq n+1.
$$

This function is bijective. Therefore $|\mathbb{N}|=|\mathbb{N}^+|$.

This is impossible for finite sets: a finite set cannot have the same cardinality as a proper subset.

## Integers Are Countably Infinite

> **Theorem.**
>
> $$
> |\mathbb{Z}|=|\mathbb{N}|.
> $$

One clean proof is to construct injections both ways and apply Cantor-Schroder-Bernstein.

The inclusion

$$
\mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{Z}
$$

is injective. In the other direction, define

$$
g:\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{N}
$$

by

$$
g(z)\coloneqq
\begin{cases}
2z, & z\geq 0,\\
2|z|-1, & z<0.
\end{cases}
$$

This function is injective. Therefore $|\mathbb{Z}|=|\mathbb{N}|$.

## Pairs of Naturals Are Countable

> **Theorem.**
>
> $$
> |\mathbb{N}\times\mathbb{N}|=|\mathbb{N}|.
> $$

The same method handles ordered pairs. The diagonal map

$$
n\mapsto (n,n)
$$

injects $\mathbb{N}$ into $\mathbb{N}\times\mathbb{N}$.

An injection in the other direction is

$$
(a,b)\mapsto 2^a3^b.
$$

Unique prime factorization makes this map injective. Cantor-Schroder-Bernstein then gives the equality of cardinalities.

## Rational Numbers Are Countable

> **Theorem.** The set $\mathbb{Q}$ is countable.

Each rational number can be represented by a pair of integers:

$$
\frac{p}{q}
\quad\text{with}\quad
p\in\mathbb{Z},\ q\in\mathbb{Z}\setminus\{0\}.
$$

The set of integer pairs is countable, and a set that can be organized by countable data is countable. Duplicate representations such as $1/2=2/4$ do not hurt this argument; extra names make a listing redundant, not impossible.

## Core Summary

Infinite cardinality is measured by functions, not by finite intuition. The sets $\mathbb{N}$, $\mathbb{N}^+$, $\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{N}\times\mathbb{N}$, and $\mathbb{Q}$ all have the same countably infinite size, even though they look quite different at first glance.
