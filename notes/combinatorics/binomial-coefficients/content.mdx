Binomial coefficients count subsets, not ordered lists. The notation $\binom{n}{k}$ records the number of ways to choose a $k$-element subset from an $n$-element set.

## Definition

> **Definition.** Let $n,k\in\mathbb{N}$. If $X$ is any finite set with $|X|=n$, then
>
> $$
> \binom{n}{k}
> \coloneqq
> |\{\,S\subseteq X\mid |S|=k\,\}|.
> $$

The identity of the elements of $X$ does not matter. Only $|X|=n$ matters.

If $k>n$, then

$$
\binom{n}{k}=0
$$

because no subset of an $n$-element set can have more than $n$ elements.

## Boundary Values

> **Proposition.** For every $n\in\mathbb{N}$,
>
> $$
> \binom{n}{0}=1
> \quad\text{and}\quad
> \binom{n}{n}=1.
> $$

There is exactly one $0$-element subset: the empty set. There is exactly one $n$-element subset of an $n$-element set: the whole set.

## Symmetry

> **Theorem.** If $n,k\in\mathbb{N}$ and $k\leq n$, then
>
> $$
> \binom{n}{k}=\binom{n}{n-k}.
> $$

**Proof.** Let $X$ be a set with $|X|=n$.

Define

$$
\phi:\{\,S\subseteq X\mid |S|=k\,\}\to \{\,T\subseteq X\mid |T|=n-k\,\}
$$

by

$$
\phi(S)\coloneqq X\setminus S.
$$

If $|S|=k$, then $|X\setminus S|=n-k$, so $\phi$ has the stated codomain.

The inverse function is the same complement operation:

$$
T\mapsto X\setminus T.
$$

Therefore $\phi$ is a bijection. Hence the two sets have the same cardinality, so

$$
\binom{n}{k}=\binom{n}{n-k}.
$$

## Pascal's Recurrence

> **Theorem.** Let $n,k\in\mathbb{N}$. Then
>
> $$
> \binom{n+1}{k+1}
> =
> \binom{n}{k+1}
> +
> \binom{n}{k}.
> $$

**Proof.** Let $X$ be a set with $|X|=n+1$, and choose $x\in X$.

Let

$$
Y\coloneqq X\setminus\{x\}.
$$

Then $|Y|=n$.

Every $(k+1)$-element subset of $X$ either contains $x$ or does not contain $x$.

Subsets that do not contain $x$ are exactly the $(k+1)$-element subsets of $Y$, and there are

$$
\binom{n}{k+1}
$$

of them.

Subsets that do contain $x$ are obtained by choosing $k$ additional elements from $Y$ and then adding $x$. There are

$$
\binom{n}{k}
$$

of them.

The two classes are disjoint and cover all $(k+1)$-element subsets of $X$. Therefore

$$
\binom{n+1}{k+1}
=
\binom{n}{k+1}
+
\binom{n}{k}.
$$

## Core Summary

The coefficient $\binom{n}{k}$ counts $k$-element subsets. Symmetry comes from taking complements. Pascal's recurrence comes from splitting subsets according to whether they contain one chosen element.
