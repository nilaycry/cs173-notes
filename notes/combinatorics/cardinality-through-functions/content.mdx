Counting in this section is not first presented as arithmetic. It is presented as the existence of functions with specific properties.

## Functions and Size

> **Definition.** Let $f:X\to Y$ be a function. The function $f$ is injective if
>
> $$
> (\forall a,b\in X)(f(a)=f(b)\Rightarrow a=b).
> $$

An injection proves that the domain can fit into the codomain without collisions.

> **Definition.** Let $f:X\to Y$ be a function. The function $f$ is surjective if
>
> $$
> (\forall y\in Y)(\exists x\in X)(f(x)=y).
> $$

A surjection proves that the domain can cover the codomain.

> **Definition.** A function is bijective if it is both injective and surjective.

## Cardinal Comparisons

> **Definition.** For sets $A$ and $B$,
>
> $$
> |A|\leq |B|
> $$
>
> means that there exists an injection $f:A\to B$.

> **Definition.** For sets $A$ and $B$,
>
> $$
> |A|\geq |B|
> $$
>
> means that there exists a surjection $g:A\to B$.

> **Definition.** For sets $A$ and $B$,
>
> $$
> |A|=|B|
> $$
>
> means that there exists a bijection $h:A\to B$.

The direction matters. An injection $A\to B$ says $A$ is no larger than $B$. A surjection $A\to B$ says $A$ is no smaller than $B$.

## Finite Sets

> **Definition.** A set $F$ is finite if there exists some $n\in\mathbb{N}$ such that
>
> $$
> |F|=|n|.
> $$

Here $n$ is treated as the finite initial segment of natural numbers with $n$ elements. The course often writes functions such as $f:n\to F$ when formally counting a finite set.

If $F$ is finite, the natural number $n$ with $|F|=|n|$ is the cardinality of $F$.

## Subsets Are No Larger

> **Proposition.** If $X\subseteq Y$, then $|X|\leq |Y|$.

**Proof.** Define

$$
i:X\to Y
$$

by

$$
i(x)\coloneqq x.
$$

This is well-defined because $X\subseteq Y$.

To show $i$ is injective, let $a,b\in X$ and suppose $i(a)=i(b)$. By definition of $i$, this means $a=b$.

Therefore $i$ is injective, so $|X|\leq |Y|$.

## Composition Preserves Comparisons

> **Proposition.** If $|A|\leq |B|$ and $|B|\leq |C|$, then $|A|\leq |C|$.

**Proof.** By definition, there exist injections

$$
f:A\to B
\quad\text{and}\quad
g:B\to C.
$$

The composition

$$
g\circ f:A\to C
$$

is injective. Indeed, if

$$
(g\circ f)(a_1)=(g\circ f)(a_2),
$$

then

$$
g(f(a_1))=g(f(a_2)).
$$

Since $g$ is injective,

$$
f(a_1)=f(a_2).
$$

Since $f$ is injective,

$$
a_1=a_2.
$$

Thus $g\circ f$ is injective, so $|A|\leq |C|$.

## Cantor-Schroder-Bernstein

> **Theorem.** If there exist injections $f:X\to Y$ and $g:Y\to X$, then $|X|=|Y|$.

This theorem is used as a size-comparison tool. Instead of constructing a bijection directly, it can be enough to construct injections in both directions.

## Core Summary

Cardinality comparisons are function statements. To prove $|A|\leq |B|$, construct an injection from $A$ to $B$. To prove $|A|\geq |B|$, construct a surjection from $A$ to $B$. To prove equality, construct a bijection or use injections both ways.
