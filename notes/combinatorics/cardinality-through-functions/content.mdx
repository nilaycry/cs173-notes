Counting in this section begins with a deceptively modest idea: to compare two sets, look for a function that witnesses the comparison. The function is the formal evidence, but the cardinality statement still has its ordinary size meaning.

## Functions and Size

Let $f:X\to Y$. The function is **injective** when distinct inputs never collide:

$$
(\forall a,b\in X)(f(a)=f(b)\Rightarrow a=b).
$$

Equivalently, $f$ is injective when unequal inputs have unequal outputs:

$$
(\forall a,b\in X)(a\neq b\Rightarrow f(a)\neq f(b)).
$$

The two forms are contrapositives of each other. In practice, the first version is often easier when proving a function is injective: assume $f(a)=f(b)$ and work toward $a=b$. The second version is often closer to the intuition: different inputs do not land on the same output.

An injection says that $X$ can be placed inside $Y$ without identifying two elements. The function is **surjective** when every output is hit:

$$
(\forall y\in Y)(\exists x\in X)(f(x)=y).
$$

A surjection says that $X$ covers all of $Y$. A **bijection** is both injective and surjective, often written informally as "inj. and surj.": no collisions and no missed outputs.

## Cardinal Comparisons

Cardinality statements are statements about relative size. The function definition tells us how the course makes those size statements precise.

$$
|A|\leq |B|
\quad\text{means } A\text{ has no more elements than }B.
$$

Formally, we prove this by giving an injection $A\to B$.

$$
|A|\geq |B|
\quad\text{means } A\text{ has at least as many elements as }B.
$$

Formally, we prove this by giving a surjection $A\to B$.

$$
|A|=|B|
\quad\text{means } A\text{ and }B\text{ have the same size.}
$$

Formally, we prove this by giving a bijection $A\to B$.

The direction is part of the content. An injection $A\to B$ is evidence that $A$ is no larger than $B$; a surjection $A\to B$ is evidence that $A$ is no smaller than $B$. The function is the witness, while the cardinality symbol records the size comparison.

## Finite Sets

A set $F$ is **finite** if there is some $n\in\mathbb{N}$ such that

$$
|F|=|n|.
$$

Here $n$ is treated as the finite initial segment of natural numbers with $n$ elements. This is why the course often writes functions such as $f:n\to F$ when formally counting a finite set.

If $F$ is finite, the natural number $n$ with $|F|=|n|$ is the cardinality of $F$.

## Subsets Are No Larger

> **Proposition.** If $X\subseteq Y$, then $|X|\leq |Y|$.

**Proof.** Define

$$
i:X\to Y
$$

by

$$
i(x)\coloneqq x.
$$

This is well-defined because $X\subseteq Y$.

To show $i$ is injective, let $a,b\in X$ and suppose $i(a)=i(b)$. By definition of $i$, this means $a=b$.

Therefore $i$ is injective, so $|X|\leq |Y|$.

## Composition Preserves Comparisons

> **Proposition.** If $|A|\leq |B|$ and $|B|\leq |C|$, then $|A|\leq |C|$.

**Proof.** By definition, there exist injections

$$
f:A\to B
\quad\text{and}\quad
g:B\to C.
$$

The composition

$$
g\circ f:A\to C
$$

is injective. Indeed, if

$$
(g\circ f)(a_1)=(g\circ f)(a_2),
$$

then

$$
g(f(a_1))=g(f(a_2)).
$$

Since $g$ is injective,

$$
f(a_1)=f(a_2).
$$

Since $f$ is injective,

$$
a_1=a_2.
$$

Thus $g\circ f$ is injective, so $|A|\leq |C|$.

## Cantor-Schroder-Bernstein

> **Theorem.** If there exist injections $f:X\to Y$ and $g:Y\to X$, then $|X|=|Y|$.

This theorem is a size-comparison tool. Instead of producing one perfect matching in a single stroke, we can sometimes squeeze $X$ into $Y$ and $Y$ back into $X$, then let the theorem supply the bijection.

## Core Summary

The moral is that cardinality comparisons are proved by functions, but they are still size comparisons. To prove $|A|\leq |B|$, build an injection $A\to B$. To prove $|A|\geq |B|$, build a surjection $A\to B$. To prove equality, either build a bijection directly by checking it is inj. and surj., or use injections in both directions with Cantor-Schroder-Bernstein.
