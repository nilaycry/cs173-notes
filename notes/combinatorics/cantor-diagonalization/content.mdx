Countably infinite sets can be listed by natural numbers. Cantor's theorem shows that some infinite sets refuse every possible list. The proof is short, but it is one of the sharpest moves in the course: build the missing object by disagreeing with the proposed list at exactly the right places.

## Power Sets and Binary Strings

Recall that the power set of $X$ is

$$
\mathcal{P}(X)\coloneqq \{\,Y\mid Y\subseteq X\,\}.
$$

Subsets of $\mathbb{N}$ correspond to infinite binary strings. Given $S\subseteq\mathbb{N}$, define

$$
\chi_S:\mathbb{N}\to\{0,1\}
$$

by

$$
\chi_S(n)=1
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
n\in S.
$$

So proving that $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})$ is uncountable is equivalent to proving that the set of infinite binary strings is uncountable.

## Cantor's Theorem

> **Theorem.** For every set $X$,
>
> $$
> |X|<|\mathcal{P}(X)|.
> $$

The strict inequality says two things at once: subsets of $X$ are at least as numerous as elements of $X$, and no function from $X$ onto $\mathcal{P}(X)$ can exist.

## Diagonal Proof

**Proof.** Let

$$
f:X\to\mathcal{P}(X)
$$

be any function. We will prove that $f$ is not surjective.

Define

$$
D\coloneqq \{\,x\in X\mid x\notin f(x)\,\}.
$$

Since $D\subseteq X$, we have $D\in\mathcal{P}(X)$.

Suppose, for contradiction, that $f$ is surjective. Then there exists $d\in X$ such that

$$
f(d)=D.
$$

Now ask the one question the definition was designed to make impossible: is $d\in D$?

By definition of $D$,

$$
d\in D
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
d\notin f(d).
$$

Since $f(d)=D$, this becomes

$$
d\in D
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
d\notin D,
$$

which is impossible.

Therefore no function $X\to\mathcal{P}(X)$ is surjective. Hence

$$
|X|<|\mathcal{P}(X)|.
$$

## Binary-String Diagonalization

If someone claims to list all infinite binary strings as

$$
\phi:\mathbb{N}\to\{\,s\mid s:\mathbb{N}\to\{0,1\}\,\},
$$

construct a new binary string $\delta:\mathbb{N}\to\{0,1\}$ by

$$
\delta(n)\coloneqq
\begin{cases}
0, & \phi(n)(n)=1,\\
1, & \phi(n)(n)=0.
\end{cases}
$$

The string $\delta$ differs from $\phi(n)$ at position $n$ for every $n\in\mathbb{N}$. Therefore $\delta$ is not in the list, contradicting that the list was complete.

## Core Summary

Diagonalization defeats a proposed listing by constructing an object that disagrees with the listed object at its own index. Cantor's theorem applies this idea to power sets, and the binary-string version proves that there are uncountably many infinite binary strings.
