Finite counting rules are cardinality statements about sets. The arithmetic is the last line, not the proof. First identify the set being counted, then explain why the pieces do not overlap or exactly how much overlap must be corrected.

## Disjoint Union Rule

> **Theorem.** If $A$ and $B$ are finite sets with $A\cap B=\varnothing$, then
>
> $$
> |A\cup B|=|A|+|B|.
> $$

This is the formal version of the sum rule. It applies when the alternatives are genuinely disjoint.

If $A$ and $B$ are not disjoint, then adding $|A|+|B|$ counts every element of $A\cap B$ twice.

## Inclusion-Exclusion for Two Sets

> **Theorem.** If $A$ and $B$ are finite sets, then
>
> $$
> |A\cup B|=|A|+|B|-|A\cap B|.
> $$

**Proof Sketch.** The sets

$$
A\setminus B,\qquad B\setminus A,\qquad A\cap B
$$

are pairwise disjoint, and their union is $A\cup B$. Therefore

$$
|A\cup B|
=
|A\setminus B|+|B\setminus A|+|A\cap B|.
$$

Also,

$$
|A|=|A\setminus B|+|A\cap B|
$$

and

$$
|B|=|B\setminus A|+|A\cap B|.
$$

Substituting shows that $|A|+|B|$ counts $A\cap B$ twice. Inclusion-exclusion removes the extra copy.

## Difference Rule

> **Corollary.** If $A$ and $B$ are finite sets with $B\subseteq A$, then
>
> $$
> |A\setminus B|=|A|-|B|.
> $$

This follows because $A$ is the disjoint union

$$
A=(A\setminus B)\cup B.
$$

## Product Rule

> **Theorem.** If $A$ and $B$ are finite sets, then
>
> $$
> |A\times B|=|A|\cdot |B|.
> $$

The product rule counts ordered independent choices. An element of $A\times B$ is an ordered pair $(a,b)$ with $a\in A$ and $b\in B$.

If a task asks for "choose one object from $A$ and one object from $B$," and both choices are independent, the object being counted is usually a Cartesian product.

## Case Splits

A counting proof should state why its cases are complete and why they do not overlap.

For a disjoint case split, write something like:

$$
X=X_1\cup X_2\cup X_3
$$

where the $X_i$ are pairwise disjoint. Then

$$
|X|=|X_1|+|X_2|+|X_3|.
$$

If the pieces are not disjoint, use inclusion-exclusion or refine the cases.

## Core Summary

The sum rule counts disjoint alternatives. Inclusion-exclusion corrects overlap. The product rule counts ordered independent choices. A complete finite counting proof names the set being counted and justifies the decomposition before doing arithmetic.
