The power set is one of the first places where counting becomes structural. Instead of naming every subset, we ask how a subset is built. For a chosen element, there are only two possibilities: the subset contains it or it does not.

## Power Sets

The **power set** of $X$ is the set of all subsets of $X$:

$$
\mathcal{P}(X)\coloneqq \{\,Y\mid Y\subseteq X\,\}.
$$

Thus the elements of $\mathcal{P}(X)$ are themselves sets.

## Main Theorem

> **Theorem.** If $X$ is finite and $|X|=n$, then
>
> $$
> |\mathcal{P}(X)|=2^n.
> $$

## Proof by Induction

> **Induction Claim.** For every $n\in\mathbb{N}$ and every set $X$, if $|X|=n$, then $|\mathcal{P}(X)|=2^n$.

### Base Case

Let $X$ be a set with $|X|=0$. Then $X=\varnothing$.

The only subset of $\varnothing$ is $\varnothing$ itself, so

$$
\mathcal{P}(X)=\{\varnothing\}.
$$

Therefore

$$
|\mathcal{P}(X)|=1=2^0.
$$

### Inductive Step

Fix $k\in\mathbb{N}$ and assume that every set with cardinality $k$ has exactly $2^k$ subsets.

Let $X$ be a set with $|X|=k+1$. Choose an element

$$
x\in X.
$$

Define

$$
Y\coloneqq X\setminus\{x\}.
$$

Then $|Y|=k$, so by the inductive hypothesis,

$$
|\mathcal{P}(Y)|=2^k.
$$

Every subset of $X$ is in exactly one of two classes:

$$
\mathcal{A}\coloneqq \{\,S\subseteq X\mid x\notin S\,\}
$$

and

$$
\mathcal{B}\coloneqq \{\,S\subseteq X\mid x\in S\,\}.
$$

The class $\mathcal{A}$ is exactly $\mathcal{P}(Y)$, so

$$
|\mathcal{A}|=2^k.
$$

The class $\mathcal{B}$ is in bijection with $\mathcal{P}(Y)$ by the function

$$
\phi:\mathcal{P}(Y)\to \mathcal{B}
$$

defined by

$$
\phi(S)\coloneqq S\cup\{x\}.
$$

The inverse sends $T\in\mathcal{B}$ to $T\setminus\{x\}$. Hence

$$
|\mathcal{B}|=2^k.
$$

Since $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ are disjoint and cover $\mathcal{P}(X)$,

$$
|\mathcal{P}(X)|=|\mathcal{A}|+|\mathcal{B}|=2^k+2^k=2^{k+1}.
$$

This completes the induction.

## Characteristic Functions

There is also a useful bijective view. A subset $S\subseteq X$ can be represented by its characteristic function

$$
\chi_S:X\to\{0,1\}
$$

where

$$
\chi_S(x)=1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad x\in S.
$$

So counting subsets is the same as counting binary choices for each element of $X$: in or out.

## Core Summary

Each element of a finite set has two possibilities in a subset: included or not included. The induction proof makes this precise one element at a time by splitting all subsets into those that contain $x$ and those that do not.
