Strings count ordered choices. This is the main distinction from combinations: a subset forgets order, while a string remembers position.

## Finite Strings

> **Definition.** Let $A$ be a set and let $n\in\mathbb{N}$. A finite string over $A$ of length $n$ is a function
>
> $$
> f:n\to A.
> $$

The set $A$ is the alphabet. The value $f(i)$ is the character in position $i$.

If $|A|=m$, then the number of strings of length $n$ over $A$ is

$$
m^n.
$$

This is the product rule applied $n$ times.

## Strings With No Repeated Characters

Suppose $A$ is finite with $|A|=m$, and suppose $k\leq m$.

A string

$$
f:k\to A
$$

has no repeated characters exactly when $f$ is injective.

The number of injective strings of length $k$ over $A$ is

$$
m(m-1)(m-2)\cdots(m-k+1).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\frac{m!}{(m-k)!}.
$$

The first position has $m$ choices, the second has $m-1$ choices, and so on until $k$ positions have been filled.

## k-Permutations

> **Definition.** Let $A$ be a finite set. A $k$-permutation of $A$ is an injective string
>
> $$
> f:k\to A.
> $$

So a $k$-permutation is an ordered selection of $k$ distinct elements from $A$.

This differs from a $k$-combination:

- a $k$-combination is a $k$-element subset;
- a $k$-permutation is an ordered list of $k$ distinct elements.

## Permutations

> **Definition.** A permutation of a set $X$ is a bijection
>
> $$
> f:X\to X.
> $$

If $|X|=n$, then the number of permutations of $X$ is

$$
n!.
$$

This is the special case of $k$-permutations where $k=n$.

## Connecting Permutations and Combinations

If $|A|=n$ and $k\leq n$, then

$$
\binom{n}{k}=\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}.
$$

Reason: first choose a $k$-element subset, then order it. Each $k$-element subset has $k!$ orderings, so

$$
\text{number of injective length-}k\text{ strings}
=
\binom{n}{k}k!.
$$

Since the left side is $n!/(n-k)!$, dividing by $k!$ gives the formula.

## Core Summary

Strings are functions from a finite domain into an alphabet. They count ordered choices. Combinations count subsets, where order is forgotten. Permutations are bijections from a set to itself, and $k$-permutations are injective strings of length $k$.
