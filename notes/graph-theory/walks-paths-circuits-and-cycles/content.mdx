Movement through a graph is represented by functions whose domains are finite initial segments of the natural numbers. This notation makes the length of a walk precise and gives a clean way to talk about repeated vertices.

## Walks

> **Definition.** Let $G$ be a graph and let $k\in\mathbb{N}$. A walk of length $k$ on $G$ is a function
>
> $$
> w:k+1\to V(G)
> $$
>
> such that, for every $i<k$,
>
> $$
> \{w(i),w(i+1)\}\in E(G).
> $$

The domain $k+1$ is the set $\{0,1,\dots,k\}$. The walk starts at $w(0)$ and ends at $w(k)$.

The length is $k$, not $k+1$, because there are $k$ consecutive pairs:

$$
(w(0),w(1)),\ (w(1),w(2)),\dots,\ (w(k-1),w(k)).
$$

Repeated vertices are allowed in a walk.

![A walk that repeats a vertex](/walk-repeats-vertex.svg)

For example, the vertex list

$$
a,b,c,b,d
$$

represents a walk of length $4$ if $\{a,b\}$, $\{b,c\}$, $\{c,b\}$, and $\{b,d\}$ are edges.

## Paths

> **Definition.** A path of length $k$ on $G$ is an injective walk
>
> $$
> p:k+1\to V(G).
> $$

Injective means

$$
(\forall i,j\in k+1)(p(i)=p(j)\Rightarrow i=j).
$$

So a path is a walk with no repeated vertices.

## Circuits

> **Definition.** A circuit of length $k$ on $G$ is a walk
>
> $$
> c:k+1\to V(G)
> $$
>
> such that $c(0)=c(k)$.

A circuit starts and ends at the same vertex. The definition of circuit does not require the intermediate vertices to be distinct.

## Cycles

> **Definition.** A cycle of length $k$ on $G$ is a circuit
>
> $$
> c:k+1\to V(G)
> $$
>
> such that the only repeated vertices are the first and last ones:
>
> $$
> (\forall i,j\in k+1)\bigl(c(i)=c(j)\Rightarrow (i=j\text{ or }\{i,j\}=\{0,k\})\bigr).
> $$

A cycle is a closed walk that behaves like a path except for returning to its starting vertex.

## Walks Can Be Shortened To Paths

> **Theorem.** Let $G$ be a graph and let $x,y\in V(G)$. If there is a walk from $x$ to $y$, then there is a path from $x$ to $y$.

**Proof.** Among all walks from $x$ to $y$, choose one of minimum length:

$$
w:k+1\to V(G).
$$

We claim that $w$ is injective. Suppose not. Then there exist indices $i,j\in k+1$ such that

$$
i<j
\quad\text{and}\quad
w(i)=w(j).
$$

Delete the closed portion of the walk from position $i$ to position $j$. More formally, define

$$
w':(k-(j-i))+1\to V(G)
$$

by

$$
w'(t)\coloneqq
\begin{cases}
w(t), & 0\leq t\leq i,\\
w(t+(j-i)), & i<t\leq k-(j-i).
\end{cases}
$$

The new function still starts at $x$ and ends at $y$. It is still a walk, because the only new adjacency to check occurs at the splice point. If $j<k$, then the new edge-condition at that point is

$$
\{w'(i),w'(i+1)\}
=
\{w(i),w(j+1)\}
=
\{w(j),w(j+1)\},
$$

which was already an edge in the original walk. If $j=k$, the shortened walk simply ends at $w(i)=w(k)=y$.

In all cases, $w'$ is a shorter walk from $x$ to $y$, contradicting the choice of $w$.

Therefore $w$ has no repeated vertices. Hence $w$ is a path from $x$ to $y$.

## Why the Distinctions Matter

- Connectedness is usually stated using paths, but walks are often easier to find first.
- A tree is defined by having no cycles, not by having no closed walks with repetition.
- A proof that "forms a cycle" must identify a circuit whose only repeated vertices are its first and last.

## Core Summary

A walk may repeat vertices. A path is an injective walk. A circuit is a walk that starts and ends at the same vertex. A cycle is a circuit with no repeated intermediate vertices. Any walk between two vertices can be shortened to a path by deleting repeated-vertex loops.
