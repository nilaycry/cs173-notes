This note introduces the basic objects used to talk about movement through a graph.

## Walks

A walk of length $k$ on a graph $G$ is a function

$$
f : \{0,1,\dots,k\} \to V(G)
$$

such that for every $i < k$,

$$
\{f(i), f(i+1)\} \in E(G).
$$

Because the domain contains both $0$ and $k$, every walk comes with a first vertex $f(0)$ and a last vertex $f(k)$.

So a walk is a sequence of vertices where each consecutive pair is joined by an edge.

The important point is that a walk may repeat vertices.

![A walk that repeats a vertex](/walk-repeats-vertex.svg)

For example, the walk

$$
a,b,c,b,d
$$

starts at $a$, ends at $d$, and repeats the vertex $b$.

## Paths

A path of length $k$ on a graph $G$ is a walk

$$
p : \{0,1,\dots,k\} \to V(G)
$$

that is **injective**. In other words,

$$
(\forall i,j \in \{0,1,\dots,k\})\bigl(p(i)=p(j)\Rightarrow i=j\bigr).
$$

So a path is a walk that does not repeat vertices.

## Length

If

$$
w : \{0,1,\dots,k\} \to V(G)
$$

is a walk, then the length of $w$ is defined to be $k$.

So the length is the number of edges used, not the number of vertices written down.

Equivalently, a walk

$$
v_0,v_1,\dots,v_k
$$

has length $k$ because it contains exactly the $k$ consecutive edge-steps

$$
\{v_0,v_1\},\{v_1,v_2\},\dots,\{v_{k-1},v_k\}.
$$

## Circuits

A circuit of length $k$ on a graph $G$ is a walk

$$
w : \{0,1,\dots,k\} \to V(G)
$$

such that

$$
w(0)=w(k).
$$

So a circuit is a walk whose first and last vertices are the same.

The definition does not require the intermediate vertices to be distinct.

## Cycles

A cycle of length $k$ on a graph $G$ is a circuit

$$
c : \{0,1,\dots,k\} \to V(G)
$$

such that the only repeated vertices are the first and last ones. A precise way to say this is:

$$
(\forall i,j \in \{0,1,\dots,k\})\bigl(c(i)=c(j)\Rightarrow (i=j \text{ or } \{i,j\}=\{0,k\})\bigr).
$$

So a cycle is a closed walk with no repeated intermediate vertices.

## Summary

- a walk may repeat vertices
- a path is an injective walk
- a circuit is a walk with $w(0)=w(k)$
- a cycle is a circuit with no repeated intermediate vertices

## Why These Distinctions Matter

A proof about connectedness usually asks for a path, not merely a walk.

A tree is defined by the absence of cycles, not by the absence of arbitrary closed walks with repetition.
