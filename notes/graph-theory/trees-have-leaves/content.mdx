## Statement

For every tree $T$,

$$
|V(T)| \geq 2 \Rightarrow (\exists v \in V(T))(\deg_T(v)=1).
$$

## Proof

Let $T$ be a tree, and assume

$$
|V(T)| \geq 2.
$$

For each $n \in \mathbb{N}$, write

$$
I_n \coloneqq \{i \in \mathbb{N} \mid i \leq n\}.
$$

Since $T$ is connected and contains at least two vertices, there exists a path in $T$ of positive length.

Now define

$$
L \coloneqq \{k \in \mathbb{N} \mid \text{there exists a path of length } k \text{ in } T\}.
$$

The set $L$ is nonempty. Also, since $T$ is finite, there are only finitely many paths in $T$, so $L$ is finite.

Therefore $L$ has a maximum element. Choose a path

$$
p : I_k \to V(T)
$$

such that $k = \max L$.

We claim that $p(0)$ is a leaf.

Since $k \geq 1$, we have

$$
\{p(0),p(1)\} \in E(T),
$$

so

$$
\deg_T(p(0)) \geq 1.
$$

It remains to show that

$$
\deg_T(p(0)) \not\geq 2.
$$

Assume, for contradiction, that

$$
\deg_T(p(0)) \geq 2.
$$

Then there exists

$$
w \in N_T(p(0))
$$

with

$$
w \neq p(1).
$$

Because $p$ is a path, it is injective, so the vertices

$$
\{p(i) \mid i \in I_k\}
$$

are pairwise distinct. Equivalently,

$$
(\forall r,s \in I_k)(r \neq s \Rightarrow p(r) \neq p(s)).
$$

We now consider two cases.

### Case 1: $(\forall i \in I_k)(w \neq p(i))$

Define

$$
q : I_{k+1} \to V(T)
$$

by

$$
q(0) \coloneqq w
\quad\text{and}\quad
q(i+1) \coloneqq p(i)
\text{ for every } i \in I_k.
$$

Then $q$ is a walk, because

- $\{q(0),q(1)\} = \{w,p(0)\} \in E(T)$ since $w \in N_T(p(0))$
- for every $i < k$, we have
  $$
  \{q(i+1),q(i+2)\} = \{p(i),p(i+1)\} \in E(T)
  $$
  since $p$ is a path

Also, $q$ is injective:

- if $r,s \in I_{k+1} \setminus \{0\}$ and $r \neq s$, then
  $$
  q(r)=p(r-1)
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  q(s)=p(s-1),
  $$
  and since $r-1 \neq s-1$, injectivity of $p$ gives
  $$
  q(r) \neq q(s)
  $$
- if $r \in I_{k+1} \setminus \{0\}$, then
  $$
  q(0)=w \neq p(r-1)=q(r)
  $$
  by the assumption of this case

So $q$ is a path of length $k+1$ in $T$.

This contradicts the maximality of $k$.

### Case 2: $(\exists i \in I_k)(w = p(i))$

Since $w \in N_T(p(0))$, we have

$$
\{p(0),w\} \in E(T).
$$

Also, $w \neq p(0)$ because no vertex is adjacent to itself in a simple graph, and $w \neq p(1)$ by construction.

Therefore there exists some integer $i$ with

$$
2 \leq i \leq k
\quad\text{and}\quad
w = p(i).
$$

Now consider the subpath of $p$ from $p(0)$ to $p(i)$.

Because $p$ is a path, the vertices

$$
\{p(j) \mid j \in I_i\}
$$

are pairwise distinct.

So the walk

$$
c : I_i \to V(T)
$$

defined by

$$
c(j) \coloneqq p(j)
\text{ for } 0 \leq j < i,
\quad\text{and}\quad
c(i) \coloneqq p(0)
$$

is a cycle:

- consecutive vertices are joined by edges, because
  - $\{p(j),p(j+1)\} \in E(T)$ for $0 \leq j < i-1$
  - $\{p(i-1),p(i)\} \in E(T)$ since $p$ is a path
  - $\{p(i),p(0)\} = \{w,p(0)\} \in E(T)$
- the first and last vertices are both $p(0)$
- no other repetition occurs, because $(\forall r,s \in I_i)(r \neq s \Rightarrow p(r) \neq p(s))$

Thus $T$ contains a cycle.

But a tree is acyclic, so this is impossible.

Both cases lead to contradictions. Therefore

$$
\deg_T(p(0)) \not\geq 2.
$$

Since we already know $\deg_T(p(0)) \geq 1$, it follows that

$$
\deg_T(p(0)) = 1.
$$

Hence $p(0)$ is a leaf of $T$.

Therefore every tree with at least two vertices contains a leaf.
