## Statement

For every tree $T$ and every pair of vertices $u,v \in V(T)$, there exists a unique path in $T$ from $u$ to $v$.

## Notation

Let

$$
w : \{0,1,\dots,k\} \to V(T)
$$

be a walk.

If $0 \leq a \leq b \leq k$, define the subwalk

$$
w[a,b] : \{0,1,\dots,b-a\} \to V(T)
$$

by

$$
w[a,b](t) \coloneqq w(a+t).
$$

If

$$
\alpha : \{0,1,\dots,m\} \to V(T)
$$

is a walk, define its reverse walk

$$
\alpha^{\mathrm{rev}} : \{0,1,\dots,m\} \to V(T)
$$

by

$$
\alpha^{\mathrm{rev}}(t) \coloneqq \alpha(m-t).
$$

If

$$
\alpha : \{0,1,\dots,m\} \to V(T)
\quad\text{and}\quad
\beta : \{0,1,\dots,n\} \to V(T)
$$

satisfy $\alpha(m)=\beta(0)$, define the concatenation

$$
\alpha * \beta : \{0,1,\dots,m+n\} \to V(T)
$$

by

$$
(\alpha * \beta)(t) \coloneqq
\begin{cases}
\alpha(t), & 0 \leq t \leq m, \\
\beta(t-m), & m \leq t \leq m+n.
\end{cases}
$$

This is well-defined because both formulas give the same value when $t=m$.

## Proof

Let $T$ be a tree, and let $u,v \in V(T)$.

We prove the statement by cases.

### Case 1: $u=v$

Define

$$
p : \{0\} \to V(T)
$$

by

$$
p(0) \coloneqq u.
$$

This is a path of length $0$ from $u$ to $u$.

Now let

$$
q : \{0,1,\dots,n\} \to V(T)
$$

be any path from $u$ to $u$. Then

$$
q(0)=u=q(n).
$$

Since $q$ is injective, it follows that $n=0$.

Hence $q$ is also the length-$0$ path at $u$, so $q=p$.

Therefore there exists a unique path from $u$ to $u$.

### Case 2: $u \neq v$

Since $T$ is connected, there exists a path in $T$ from $u$ to $v$.

So only uniqueness remains to be proved.

Assume, for contradiction, that there are two distinct paths from $u$ to $v$.

Let

$$
p : \{0,1,\dots,m\} \to V(T)
$$

and

$$
q : \{0,1,\dots,n\} \to V(T)
$$

be paths such that

$$
p(0)=u,\qquad p(m)=v,\qquad q(0)=u,\qquad q(n)=v.
$$

Since $p$ and $q$ are distinct, there exists a smallest index $i$ with

$$
i \leq \min\{m,n\}
\quad\text{and}\quad
p(i) \neq q(i).
$$

Because $p(0)=q(0)=u$, we have $i \geq 1$.

Set

$$
r \coloneqq i-1.
$$

Then

$$
p(r)=q(r),
$$

and this is the last index before the two paths diverge.

Now define

$$
S \coloneqq \{t \in \{i,i+1,\dots,m\} \mid (\exists s \in \{i,i+1,\dots,n\})(p(t)=q(s))\}.
$$

The set $S$ is nonempty because

$$
p(m)=v=q(n).
$$

Let

$$
j \coloneqq \min S.
$$

Then there exists some $\ell \in \{i,i+1,\dots,n\}$ such that

$$
p(j)=q(\ell).
$$

Since $q$ is injective, this $\ell$ is unique.

Define

$$
\alpha \coloneqq p[r,j]
\quad\text{and}\quad
\beta \coloneqq q[r,\ell].
$$

Then:

- $\alpha(0)=p(r)$
- $\alpha(j-r)=p(j)$
- $\beta(0)=q(r)=p(r)$
- $\beta(\ell-r)=q(\ell)=p(j)$

So $\alpha$ and $\beta$ have the same start and end vertices.

Now consider the walk

$$
c \coloneqq \alpha * \beta^{\mathrm{rev}}.
$$

We claim that $c$ is a cycle.

First, $c$ is a circuit, because

$$
c(0)=\alpha(0)=p(r)=q(r)=\beta(0)=\beta^{\mathrm{rev}}(\ell-r)=c\bigl((j-r)+(\ell-r)\bigr).
$$

It remains to show that $c$ has no repeated vertices other than its first and last ones.

The walk $\alpha$ has no repeated vertices because it is a subwalk of the path $p$.

The walk $\beta$ has no repeated vertices because it is a subwalk of the path $q$.

So it is enough to show that $\alpha$ and $\beta$ have no common internal vertex.

Suppose there exist indices $a,b$ such that

$$
1 \leq a \leq j-r-1,
\qquad
1 \leq b \leq \ell-r-1,
\qquad
\alpha(a)=\beta(b).
$$

By the definition of $\alpha$ and $\beta$, this means

$$
p(r+a)=q(r+b).
$$

Since $1 \leq a$, we have $r+a \geq i$. Since $a \leq j-r-1$, we have $r+a < j$.

Similarly, $1 \leq b$ implies $r+b \geq i$.

So

$$
r+a \in \{i,i+1,\dots,j-1\}
$$

and

$$
r+b \in \{i,i+1,\dots,n\}.
$$

This means $r+a \in S$ and $r+a < j$, contradicting the minimality of $j$.

Therefore $\alpha$ and $\beta$ have no common internal vertex.

So $c$ is a cycle in $T$.

But a tree is acyclic, so $T$ contains no cycles. This is a contradiction.

Hence there cannot be two distinct paths from $u$ to $v$.

Therefore there exists a unique path from $u$ to $v$.

Since both cases have been proved, the theorem follows.
