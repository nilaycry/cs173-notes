## Statement

For every graph $G$,

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v) = 2|E(G)|.
$$

This is the first major counting theorem in the graph theory section. It connects the local data of degrees to the global data of the edge set.

## Proof Strategy

A short intuitive proof exists: every edge contributes $1$ to the degree of each of its two endpoints, so every edge is counted twice in the total degree sum.

For this course, though, it is useful to see a fully formal proof.

A natural first thought is to try induction. There are two obvious quantities one might induct on:

- $|V(G)|$, the order of the graph
- $|E(G)|$, the size of the graph

Here, induction on $|V(G)|$ works best, because deleting one vertex gives a smaller graph whose degrees can be compared directly to those of the original graph.

## Proof by Induction on $|V(G)|$

We will prove

$$
(\forall n \in \mathbb{N})(\forall G)\Bigl((G \text{ is a graph} \wedge |V(G)| = n)\Rightarrow 2|E(G)| = \sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)\Bigr).
$$

### Base Case

Assume $|V(G)| = 0$. Then $V(G) = \varnothing$.

Every edge of a graph must be a two-element subset of $V(G)$. Since $\varnothing$ has no two-element subsets, it follows that $E(G) = \varnothing$.

Therefore,

$$
2|E(G)| = 2\cdot 0 = 0
$$

and

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)
=
\sum_{v \in \varnothing} \deg_G(v)
= 0.
$$

So the statement holds when $|V(G)| = 0$.

### Inductive Hypothesis

Fix $k \in \mathbb{N}$ and assume that for every graph $H$ with $|V(H)| = k$,

$$
\sum_{v \in V(H)} \deg_H(v) = 2|E(H)|.
$$

### Inductive Step

Let $G$ be a graph with $|V(G)| = k+1$.

Since $k+1 > 0$, the vertex set is nonempty. So we may choose a vertex $x \in V(G)$.

Define a smaller graph $H$ by

$$
V(H) \coloneqq V(G)\setminus \{x\}
$$

and

$$
E(H) \coloneqq E(G)\setminus I_G(x)
=
\{e \in E(G) \mid x \notin e\}.
$$

So $H$ is obtained by deleting the vertex $x$ and every edge incident to $x$.

## Comparing Degrees in $G$ and $H$

### Vertices Not Adjacent to $x$

Let

$$
v \in V(G)\setminus (N_G(x)\cup \{x\}).
$$

Then

$$
\deg_G(v)
=
|I_G(v)|
=
\bigl|\{e \in E(G) \mid v \in e\}\bigr|.
$$

Since $v \notin N_G(x)$, there is no edge containing both $v$ and $x$. So every edge in the set above automatically satisfies $x \notin e$. Hence

$$
\deg_G(v)
=
\bigl|\{e \in E(G) \mid v \in e \wedge x \notin e\}\bigr|.
$$

By the definition of $E(H)$, this is

$$
\deg_G(v)
=
\bigl|\{e \in E(H) \mid v \in e\}\bigr|
=
|I_H(v)|
=
\deg_H(v).
$$

### Vertices Adjacent to $x$

Let $v \in N_G(x)$.

Then

$$
\deg_G(v)
=
|I_G(v)|
=
\bigl|\{e \in E(G) \mid v \in e\}\bigr|.
$$

Split this set into two disjoint pieces:

$$
\{e \in E(G) \mid v \in e \wedge x \in e\}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\{e \in E(G) \mid v \in e \wedge x \notin e\}.
$$

So

$$
\deg_G(v)
=
\bigl|\{e \in E(G) \mid v \in e \wedge x \in e\}\bigr|
+
\bigl|\{e \in E(G) \mid v \in e \wedge x \notin e\}\bigr|.
$$

Because the graph is simple and $v$ is adjacent to $x$, there is exactly one edge containing both $v$ and $x$, namely $\{v,x\}$. Therefore

$$
\bigl|\{e \in E(G) \mid v \in e \wedge x \in e\}\bigr| = 1.
$$

Also, by the definition of $E(H)$,

$$
\bigl|\{e \in E(G) \mid v \in e \wedge x \notin e\}\bigr|
=
\bigl|\{e \in E(H) \mid v \in e\}\bigr|
=
|I_H(v)|
=
\deg_H(v).
$$

Hence

$$
\deg_G(v) = 1 + \deg_H(v).
$$

## Splitting the Total Degree Sum

The vertex set $V(G)$ is the disjoint union

$$
V(G)
=
N_G(x)
\cup
\bigl(V(G)\setminus (N_G(x)\cup \{x\})\bigr)
\cup
\{x\}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)
=
\sum_{v \in N_G(x)} \deg_G(v)
+
\sum_{v \in V(G)\setminus (N_G(x)\cup \{x\})} \deg_G(v)
+
\deg_G(x).
$$

Now substitute the two degree comparisons:

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)
=
\sum_{v \in N_G(x)} \bigl(1+\deg_H(v)\bigr)
+
\sum_{v \in V(G)\setminus (N_G(x)\cup \{x\})} \deg_H(v)
+
\deg_G(x).
$$

Expand the first sum:

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)
=
\sum_{v \in N_G(x)} 1
+
\sum_{v \in N_G(x)} \deg_H(v)
+
\sum_{v \in V(G)\setminus (N_G(x)\cup \{x\})} \deg_H(v)
+
\deg_G(x).
$$

Since there is exactly one term $1$ for each vertex in $N_G(x)$,

$$
\sum_{v \in N_G(x)} 1 = |N_G(x)|.
$$

Also, because

$$
V(H)=V(G)\setminus \{x\}
=
N_G(x)\cup \bigl(V(G)\setminus (N_G(x)\cup \{x\})\bigr),
$$

the two sums involving $\deg_H$ combine to

$$
\sum_{v \in V(H)} \deg_H(v).
$$

So we obtain

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)
=
|N_G(x)|
+
\sum_{v \in V(H)} \deg_H(v)
+
\deg_G(x).
$$

In a simple graph, each neighbor of $x$ corresponds to exactly one edge incident to $x$. Therefore

$$
|N_G(x)| = |I_G(x)| = \deg_G(x).
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)
=
\deg_G(x)
+
\sum_{v \in V(H)} \deg_H(v)
+
\deg_G(x)
=
2\deg_G(x) + \sum_{v \in V(H)} \deg_H(v).
$$

By the inductive hypothesis,

$$
\sum_{v \in V(H)} \deg_H(v) = 2|E(H)|.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)
=
2\deg_G(x) + 2|E(H)|.
$$

## Recovering $|E(G)|$

The edges of $G$ split into two disjoint parts:

- the edges incident to $x$, namely $I_G(x)$
- the edges not incident to $x$, namely $E(H)$

So

$$
|E(G)| = |I_G(x)| + |E(H)|.
$$

Since $|I_G(x)| = \deg_G(x)$,

$$
|E(G)| = \deg_G(x) + |E(H)|.
$$

Multiplying by $2$ gives

$$
2|E(G)| = 2\deg_G(x) + 2|E(H)|.
$$

Hence

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v) = 2|E(G)|.
$$

This completes the inductive step, and therefore the proof.
