The first graph theorem in the course is a pigeonhole theorem in graph language. It says that the degree function cannot assign a different degree to every vertex.

## Statement

> **Theorem.** Let $G$ be a graph with $|V(G)|=n$ and $n\geq 2$. Then there exist distinct vertices $u,v\in V(G)$ such that
>
> $$
> \deg_G(u)=\deg_G(v).
> $$

This is the graph-theory translation of the handshake theorem: in any group of at least two people, two people have shaken the same number of hands.

## Degree Values

For every vertex $v\in V(G)$,

$$
0\leq \deg_G(v)\leq n-1.
$$

So the broad set of possible degree values is

$$
D\coloneqq \{\, d\in\mathbb{N}\mid d\leq n-1\,\}.
$$

This set has $n$ elements, the same number as $V(G)$. Pigeonhole cannot be applied directly to a function $V(G)\to D$.

The useful observation is that the actual degree values cannot include both extremes.

> **Lemma.** If $G$ has a vertex of degree $0$, then $G$ has no vertex of degree $n-1$.

This means that a degree value of $0$ and a degree value of $n-1$ cannot both occur.

## Proof

Let

$$
B\coloneqq \{\, \deg_G(v) \mid v\in V(G) \,\}
$$

be the set of actual degree values appearing in $G$.

Define

$$
f:V(G)\to B
$$

by

$$
f(v)\coloneqq \deg_G(v).
$$

By definition of $B$, this function is onto $B$. To prove that two vertices have the same degree, it is enough to prove that $f$ is not injective.

Since every degree is between $0$ and $n-1$, we have

$$
B\subseteq \{\, d\in\mathbb{N}\mid d\leq n-1\,\}.
$$

The values $0$ and $n-1$ cannot both belong to $B$. Therefore $B$ is a proper subset of a set with $n$ elements, so

$$
|B|\leq n-1.
$$

But

$$
|V(G)|=n>|B|.
$$

By the pigeonhole principle, $f$ is not injective. Hence there exist distinct vertices $u,v\in V(G)$ such that

$$
f(u)=f(v).
$$

By the definition of $f$,

$$
\deg_G(u)=\deg_G(v).
$$

This proves the theorem.

## Why the Codomain Matters

Using

$$
f:V(G)\to \{\, d\in\mathbb{N}\mid d\leq n-1\,\}
$$

does not immediately prove the theorem, because the domain and codomain both have size $n$.

The proof needs the smaller set of actual degree values $B$. The impossible-pair observation proves that this actual codomain has at most $n-1$ elements.

## Core Summary

The degree function sends vertices to numbers. The broad set of values $d\in\mathbb{N}$ with $d\leq n-1$ is too large for pigeonhole, but the graph cannot realize both degree $0$ and degree $n-1$. Therefore the actual degree values fit into at most $n-1$ slots, so two vertices must share a degree.
