## Statement

Every graph has an even number of odd-degree vertices.

## Proof

Let

$$
O \coloneqq \{v \in V(G) \mid \deg_G(v) \text{ is odd}\}
$$

and

$$
E \coloneqq \{v \in V(G) \mid \deg_G(v) \text{ is even}\}.
$$

Then

$$
V(G) = O \cup E
$$

and the union is disjoint. Therefore,

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)
=
\sum_{v \in O} \deg_G(v)
+
\sum_{v \in E} \deg_G(v).
$$

By the degree-sum theorem,

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v) = 2|E(G)|,
$$

so the left-hand side is even.

Also,

$$
\sum_{v \in E} \deg_G(v)
$$

is even, because it is a sum of even integers.

Therefore,

$$
\sum_{v \in O} \deg_G(v)
$$

must also be even.

But each term in that sum is odd. A sum of odd integers is even only when there are an even number of terms. Hence $|O|$ is even.

Therefore the graph has an even number of odd-degree vertices.

## Use

This theorem is often used to rule out the existence of a graph with a proposed degree pattern.
