The degree-sum theorem immediately gives a parity fact about degree values. The result is often used to rule out proposed degree patterns.

## Statement

> **Theorem.** Every graph has an even number of odd-degree vertices.

Equivalently, if

$$
O\coloneqq \{\,v\in V(G)\mid \deg_G(v)\text{ is odd}\,\},
$$

then $|O|$ is even.

## Proof

Let

$$
O \coloneqq \{v \in V(G) \mid \deg_G(v) \text{ is odd}\}
$$

and

$$
R \coloneqq \{v \in V(G) \mid \deg_G(v) \text{ is even}\}.
$$

Then $V(G)=O\cup R$, and this union is disjoint. Therefore

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)
=
\sum_{v \in O} \deg_G(v)
+
\sum_{v \in R} \deg_G(v).
$$

By the degree-sum theorem,

$$
\sum_{v \in V(G)} \deg_G(v)=2|E(G)|,
$$

so the total degree sum is even.

Also,

$$
\sum_{v \in R} \deg_G(v)
$$

is even, because it is a sum of even integers. Therefore

$$
\sum_{v \in O} \deg_G(v)
$$

must be even.

Each term in this last sum is odd. A sum of odd integers is even exactly when the number of summands is even. Hence $|O|$ is even.

Therefore every graph has an even number of odd-degree vertices.

## How the Theorem Is Used

This theorem can disprove a proposed graph.

For example, no graph can have exactly three odd-degree vertices. The exact degree values do not matter. The parity count alone is enough.

## Core Summary

The degree-sum theorem says that the total degree sum is even. Even-degree vertices contribute an even amount to that sum, so the odd-degree vertices must also contribute an even amount. That can happen only when the number of odd-degree vertices is even.
