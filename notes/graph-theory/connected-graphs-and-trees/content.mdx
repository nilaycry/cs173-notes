## Connected Graphs

A graph $G$ is connected if

$$
(\forall v,w \in V(G))(v \neq w \Rightarrow \text{there exists a path in } G \text{ from } v \text{ to } w).
$$

So connectedness is a statement about every pair of distinct vertices.

## Disconnected Graphs

A graph $G$ is disconnected if it is not connected. Equivalently,

$$
(\exists v,w \in V(G))(v \neq w \wedge \text{there is no path in } G \text{ from } v \text{ to } w).
$$

## Components

A component of $G$ is a maximal connected subgraph of $G$.

When a graph is disconnected, its components are the connected pieces it breaks into.

## Trees

A tree is a graph that is both:

- connected
- acyclic

Here acyclic means that the graph contains no cycle.

So $G$ is a tree if and only if

$$
G \text{ is connected } \wedge G \text{ has no cycles}.
$$

## Leaves

A leaf of a graph is a vertex of degree $1$.

Formally, $v$ is a leaf if

$$
v \in V(G) \quad\text{and}\quad \deg_G(v)=1.
$$

## Standard Tree Facts

The following statements appear repeatedly:

- every tree with at least two vertices has a leaf
- if $G$ is a tree and $|V(G)| = n$, then $|E(G)| = n-1$
- removing a leaf from a tree produces a smaller tree

These are the facts most often used in induction proofs about trees.
