## Statement

> **Theorem.** For every tree $T$,
>
> $$
> V(T) \neq \varnothing \Rightarrow |V(T)| = |E(T)| + 1.
> $$

## Proof Strategy

We prove the statement by induction on the order of the tree, that is, on $|V(T)|$.

The key structural move is to remove a leaf. That decreases the vertex set by one and the edge set by one, and the remaining graph is still a tree.

## Proof

> **Induction Claim.** For every $n\in\mathbb{N}$ and every tree $T$, if $|V(T)|=n+1$, then
>
> $$
> |V(T)|=|E(T)|+1.
> $$

Equivalently, we prove

$$
(\forall n \in \mathbb{N})(\forall T)\bigl((T \text{ is a tree} \wedge |V(T)| = n+1) \Rightarrow |V(T)| = |E(T)| + 1\bigr)
$$

by induction on $n$.

### Base Case

Let $T$ be a tree with

$$
|V(T)| = 1.
$$

Since every edge of a graph is a two-element subset of the vertex set, a graph with only one vertex cannot have any edges. Hence

$$
|E(T)| = 0.
$$

Therefore

$$
|V(T)| = 1 = 0 + 1 = |E(T)| + 1.
$$

So the statement holds when $n=0$.

### Inductive Hypothesis

> **Inductive Hypothesis.** Fix $k \in \mathbb{N}$ and assume that for every tree $H$,
>
> $$
> |V(H)| = k+1 \Rightarrow |V(H)| = |E(H)| + 1.
> $$

### Inductive Step

Let $T$ be a tree with

$$
|V(T)| = k+2.
$$

Then

$$
|V(T)| \geq 2,
$$

so the previous theorem implies that $T$ contains a leaf. Choose a leaf

$$
v \in V(T).
$$

Since $v$ is a leaf,

$$
\deg_T(v)=1.
$$

Let $e$ be the unique edge incident to $v$, and define a graph $H$ by

$$
V(H) \coloneqq V(T) \setminus \{v\}
$$

and

$$
E(H) \coloneqq E(T) \setminus \{e\}.
$$

We claim that $H$ is a tree.

First, $H$ is acyclic, because removing a vertex and an edge cannot create a cycle.

It remains to show that $H$ is connected.

Let $x,y \in V(H)$ with $x \neq y$. Since $T$ is connected, there exists a path

$$
p : m+1 \to V(T)
$$

from $x$ to $y$ in $T$.

Write

$$
\operatorname{Im}(p) \coloneqq \{p(i) \mid i \in m+1\}
$$

for the set of vertices visited by $p$.

We claim that $v \notin \operatorname{Im}(p)$.

Suppose, for contradiction, that $v \in \operatorname{Im}(p)$. Then there exists $i < m+1$ with

$$
p(i)=v.
$$

Since $x,y \in V(H)$, we have $x \neq v$ and $y \neq v$, so

$$
i \neq 0
\quad\text{and}\quad
i \neq m.
$$

Therefore

$$
0 < i < m.
$$

Because $p$ is a path, both

$$
\{p(i-1),p(i)\} \in E(T)
\quad\text{and}\quad
\{p(i),p(i+1)\} \in E(T).
$$

Since $p(i)=v$, this means both of those edges are incident to $v$.

Also, because $p$ is injective,

$$
p(i-1) \neq p(i+1),
$$

so these are two distinct edges incident to $v$. That contradicts

$$
\deg_T(v)=1.
$$

So indeed

$$
v \notin \operatorname{Im}(p).
$$

Hence every vertex used by $p$ lies in $V(H)$, and every edge used by $p$ lies in $E(H)$ because the only removed edge was the unique edge incident to $v$.

Therefore $p$ is also a path in $H$ from $x$ to $y$.

So $H$ is connected. Thus $H$ is a tree.

Now

$$
|V(H)| = |V(T)| - 1 = (k+2)-1 = k+1.
$$

So the inductive hypothesis applies to $H$, and we obtain

$$
|V(H)| = |E(H)| + 1.
$$

Since one vertex and one edge were removed,

$$
|V(T)| = |V(H)| + 1
$$

and

$$
|E(T)| = |E(H)| + 1.
$$

Therefore

$$
|V(T)| = (|E(H)| + 1) + 1 = |E(H)| + 2 = (|E(T)| - 1) + 2 = |E(T)| + 1.
$$

This completes the inductive step.

Hence every nonempty tree $T$ satisfies

$$
|V(T)| = |E(T)| + 1.
$$

## Core Summary

The proof removes a leaf. Removing a leaf from a tree removes exactly one vertex and exactly one edge, and the remaining graph is still a tree. The inductive hypothesis applies to the smaller tree, and the equal one-step change in vertices and edges preserves the equation $|V(T)|=|E(T)|+1$.
